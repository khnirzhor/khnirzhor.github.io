When Anonymous Isn't Anonymous: Ethical Flaws in a Course Evaluation Survey

Kamrul Hassan Nirzhor | Jul 10, 2026 min read

Introduction

Course evaluation surveys can be useful tools for improving education, but only if the information is collected honestly, fairly, and responsibly. In this scenario, students complete a ten-question survey that is described as anonymous, yet they must first log in with their organizational username and password. Management then plans to use the results to decide which courses need to be redesigned.

On the surface this looks like a routine feedback process. Underneath, it raises ethical concerns involving anonymity, informed consent, transparency, privacy, fairness, and data quality. The sample question itself also creates problems that could make the results unreliable. If students do not trust the survey, the data will not reflect their real experiences.

The Contradiction Between Login and Anonymity

The most obvious problem is that the survey requires a login while promising anonymity. When students authenticate, the system can record their username, IP address, timestamps, and course registration. Even if a name never appears beside the answers, this information could make it possible to identify who said what.

This is really the difference between anonymity and confidentiality. Anonymity means the organization cannot reasonably link a response to a person. Confidentiality means the organization can identify respondents but promises to protect that information. This survey may be confidential, but it is not anonymous, and describing it as anonymous is misleading. That is a transparency problem: students are not being given an accurate picture of how their data is handled.

The practical consequence is response bias. A student who fears being identified may soften negative feedback or skip comments entirely. Management could then conclude a course is performing well when students were simply afraid to criticize it. Poor ethical design directly produces poor-quality data.

Problems With Informed Consent

Informed consent means people understand what is collected, why, how it will be used, and what the risks are before they agree to participate. Here, students are told only that responses are anonymous. They are not told what login information is stored, who can access raw responses, how long data is kept, whether instructors will see individual comments, or whether participation is truly voluntary.

The short response window adds pressure, and if students believe the survey is required, they have no meaningful choice. Students should be clearly told that participation is voluntary and that declining will not affect their grades or standing.

Problems With the Sample Question

The sample question asks students to rate the statement “This course met all of the course objectives” from 0 (strongly disagree) to 10 (strongly agree). Two things are wrong with it.

First, the statement is too broad. A course has many objectives, and the word “all” forces one rating onto several separate issues. If a student answers six, management cannot tell whether some objectives were missed, whether they were unclear, or whether the student even knew what they were.

Second, the 0-to-10 scale invites inconsistent interpretation. One student treats seven as positive; another treats it as average. A five-point scale (strongly disagree to strongly agree) is clearer, and the single broad statement should be split into specific ones, such as:

  • The course learning objectives were clearly explained.
  • The course activities helped me achieve the learning objectives.
  • The assessments reflected the stated learning objectives.

These would give management information it can actually act on.

Other Ethical Issues

Data minimization. The organization should collect only what it needs. Deciding which courses to redesign does not require usernames, IP addresses, or detailed login records, and storing them creates unnecessary privacy and security risk.

Purpose limitation. Data collected to improve courses should not later be used to discipline instructors or evaluate individual students. Using data for a new purpose without notice is purpose creep.

Re-identification. Removing names does not guarantee anonymity, especially in small classes. If only four students took a course and one left strongly negative feedback, an instructor can guess who wrote it. Written comments can also contain identifying details. Results for very small groups should not be reported.

Fairness and representation. A short response window excludes students who are sick, working, or facing technical problems, which creates non-response bias. The survey should also be accessible to students using screen readers, mobile devices, and other assistive tools.

Security and retention. The organization should know who can access raw responses, whether the data is encrypted, and when it will be deleted. Keeping survey data indefinitely is unnecessary and risky.

Recommendations to Executive Management

First, describe the survey honestly. If the system can identify respondents, call it confidential, and publish a short privacy notice explaining what is collected, who can access it, and how long it is kept.

Second, separate authentication from responses. The login can confirm eligibility and prevent duplicate submissions, then issue a random survey token so the response is never stored with the student’s identity.

Third, redesign the questions using specific statements and a consistent five-point scale, with optional open-ended questions such as “What part of the course should be improved?”

Fourth, set a minimum reporting threshold so results are not released for very small groups, and give instructors and executives summarized results rather than raw records.

Finally, test the survey for accessibility, clarity, and security before it is distributed.

What I Would Do if Management Rejected My Recommendations

If management insisted there were no ethical problems, I would still have a professional responsibility to act ethically. I would document my concerns and recommendations in writing, and I would state the limitations clearly in my analysis rather than presenting the results as reliable. For example, instead of writing “students were satisfied with the course,” I would write that responses were positive among students who participated, but that the login requirement and unknown non-response rate limit how much confidence we can place in that conclusion.

I would refuse to make claims the data cannot support, and I would not release raw comments, usernames, or small-group breakdowns that could identify students. If the concerns were serious, I could raise them with a privacy officer, data governance team, or ethics committee. And if I were asked to hide known risks or mislead students, I would consider refusing that part of the work, because participating in a harmful process can be worse than withdrawing from it.

Conclusion

The core problem in this scenario is that the promise of anonymity conflicts with the requirement to log in, and the sample question produces scores that cannot be interpreted. The organization should separate authentication from responses, explain its privacy practices, collect only what it needs, improve the questions, protect small groups, and report findings honestly.

Most importantly, ethics is not separate from data quality. A survey is not anonymous just because it does not ask for a name; the promise has to be backed by the technology, the data handling, and the reporting. When students do not trust a survey, they do not answer honestly, and the organization ends up making decisions on misleading information.
