The Algorithm Said Your House Is Worth What? Bias, Accountability, and the Limits of AI Property Valuation

Kamrul Hassan Nirzhor | Jul 13, 2026 min read

Imagine checking an online real estate platform and discovering that an algorithm has valued your home tens of thousands of dollars below what similar properties are selling for in your neighbourhood. You know your house has a finished basement, a renovated kitchen, and a premium lot, but the computer treats it like every other house on the street. Now imagine the opposite: the algorithm overvalues the home, a company buys it based on that estimate, and later realizes it cannot resell the property without taking a loss.

This is not hypothetical. In 2021, Zillow shut down Zillow Offers, its technology-driven home-buying business, after struggling to predict future home prices accurately. Zillow recorded approximately US$407.9 million in inventory write-downs for 2021, largely because it had unintentionally purchased homes for more than it later expected to receive after resale costs. The shutdown was expected to reduce Zillow’s workforce by roughly 25%.

When an organization relies on an algorithm to make real purchasing decisions, valuation errors affect homeowners, employees, investors, and neighbourhoods. That raises the question at the heart of this post: when an algorithm gets a property valuation wrong, who should be held responsible?

As a REALTOR® working in the Greater Toronto Area, I regularly see the difference between an automated estimate and a comparative market analysis completed by someone who understands the local market. This case study examines Zillow’s failure through several AI ethics concepts, including algorithmic bias, transparency, explainability, accountability, human oversight, data quality, AI governance, and soft ethics.

What Is an Automated Valuation Model?

An automated valuation model, or AVM, is a computer-based system that estimates the value of a property. The Appraisal Institute of Canada defines an AVM as a program that analyzes data through processes that may include regression, adaptive estimation, neural networks, expert reasoning, and artificial intelligence. An AVM typically considers previous sale prices, recent comparable sales, property size and age, lot dimensions, tax and public records, listing history, and neighbourhood trends.

The advantages are real: an AVM reviews thousands of properties faster than any person, produces an estimate instantly, and applies a consistent calculation. But speed and consistency do not guarantee accuracy or fairness. An AVM can only work with the information it receives. This is the principle of data quality: if the data is incomplete, outdated, or unable to represent important features, the valuation will be misleading no matter how sophisticated the model is.

A computer might recognize that two houses are both detached, four-bedroom properties with similar square footage. It may not understand that one sits beside a busy road while the other backs onto a ravine, that one basement was finished with permits while the other needs major repairs, or that a school boundary runs between them. In a competitive market, these details significantly change what a buyer will pay.

From Zestimate to Zillow Offers

Zillow’s best-known tool is the Zestimate, an automated estimate of a home’s market value built on public records, listing data, artificial intelligence, computer vision, and deep learning. In early 2021, Zillow reported a nationwide median error rate of about 1.9% for on-market homes.

That sounds accurate until you do the math. On a $1 million property, 1.9% is $19,000 — and a median error means half of all estimates were off by more than that, sometimes far more, particularly for unusual homes or data-poor areas.

Zillow went beyond displaying estimates. Through Zillow Offers, it purchased homes directly from owners, renovated them, and attempted to resell them, with initial cash offers in some markets based on the Zestimate. To be fair, the failure was not caused by the public Zestimate alone: Zillow’s filings describe proprietary pricing algorithms plus in-person evaluations, and the company cited home-price unpredictability, renovation and labour challenges, the pandemic, and supply-chain problems in the shutdown. But the business fundamentally depended on using data and forecasting systems to answer a hard question: what will this house be worth after we buy it, repair it, carry it, and resell it? Zillow was not estimating current value — it was predicting future value at scale. When those predictions failed, the errors became extremely expensive.

The lesson is a basic ethical principle: the scale of an AI system multiplies the scale of its errors. A real estate agent who misjudges one property loses one listing. A company using an automated system to purchase thousands of properties can repeat the same mistake across an entire portfolio before recognizing the pattern. Automation turns an individual error into a systemic risk.

Bias Is Not Always About Personal Identity

When people hear algorithmic bias, they usually think of discrimination involving race, gender, or age. Those are serious concerns, but bias also appears through geography, property type, and data availability. In property valuation, a model may be systematically less accurate for neighbourhoods with fewer recent sales, unusual or custom-built homes, properties with unrecorded improvements, rapidly changing local markets, and homes with legal or illegal secondary units.

A model that performs well in a subdivision of near-identical houses may perform poorly on a century home, a renovated bungalow with an addition, or a property on the border of two very different school zones. This creates data availability bias: homes that fit common patterns get reliable estimates, while uncommon properties get educated guesses — and the system presents both numbers with the same confident face. Users have no way to know which estimate rests on strong evidence and which one is barely supported.

What a GTA CMA Sees That an AVM Misses

In the GTA, value changes within a very small geographic area. When I prepare a comparative market analysis, I am not averaging sale prices — I am asking why each comparable sold for what it did. One comparable was fully renovated while another sold in original condition. One had a legal basement apartment; another only finished space. One drew multiple offers because it was priced low for an offer night; another sold under market because it was tenanted and difficult to show. One backed onto green space; the subject backs onto a commercial building. One sold the week before an interest-rate announcement shifted buyer confidence.

An AVM sees the final numbers without the story behind them.

This does not mean humans are automatically better. Agents can cherry-pick comparables to win a listing, overweight their own experience, or tell sellers what they want to hear — human bias is real. The ethical answer is not “humans good, algorithms bad.” It is a system that combines the processing power of technology with local knowledge, professional judgment, and clear accountability.

Transparency and Explainability

Transparency means users should understand when an AI system is being used, what information it considers, and what its limitations are. A homeowner who sees a precise figure like $947,350 naturally assumes it is reliable because it looks specific. But precision is not accuracy — that number may rest on incomplete records and weak comparables. An ethical valuation tool should communicate a reasonable range, the age and quality of the data, the model’s confidence level, and whether a human reviewed the result. Without that, users fall into automation bias: giving excessive weight to a computer-generated number simply because it appears neutral and mathematical.

Explainability goes a step further. Transparency tells us a model is being used; explainability tells us how it reached its conclusion. If an AVM says $900,000 while a local analysis says closer to $1 million, the homeowner should be able to ask: which comparables were selected? Did the model know about the finished basement? Was it pulling sales from a different micro-market? A valuation that cannot be explained cannot be challenged — which matters enormously when the number affects a mortgage, a refinancing application, or a cash offer. In Canada, OSFI’s mortgage-underwriting guidance expects federally regulated lenders to take a risk-based approach to collateral valuation and holds institutions and senior management responsible for oversight. The broader principle: a high-impact valuation should never become unquestionable merely because a computer produced it.

Accountability: The Algorithm Cannot Take Responsibility

The central ethical problem in the Zillow case is accountability. When the predictions failed, who was responsible — the data scientists who built the model, the executives who approved rapid expansion, the managers buying homes, or market conditions nobody could predict?

The honest answer involves all of them. But one answer is clearly unacceptable: “the algorithm made the mistake.” An algorithm cannot apologize, compensate anyone, or accept legal and moral responsibility. The organization that chooses to deploy the algorithm remains accountable. Zillow’s leadership decided to connect automated estimates to cash offers, to purchase at scale, and to keep expanding. Even when the model underperformed, responsibility stayed with the people who decided how much authority to give it.

Ethical AI governance therefore requires clear ownership before launch: who validates accuracy, who monitors performance when markets shift, who can pause or override the system, who answers a homeowner who disputes a valuation, and who is accountable when the system causes financial harm. Without named answers, responsibility spreads so thin that no one holds it.

Human oversight is often proposed as the safeguard, but placing a person somewhere in the process is not enough. If an employee is expected to approve hundreds of algorithmic valuations a day, review becomes a formality. Meaningful oversight means inspecting unusual properties, flagging low-confidence valuations, and recognizing when changing conditions have made past data unreliable. The model should be a tool, not the final decision-maker.

Governance and Soft Ethics

Regulation sets minimum rules, but it rarely keeps pace with new uses of AI. Real estate valuation is a good example: no regulator told Zillow how much authority its forecasting models could safely carry. This is where soft ethics matters — the voluntary standards, internal review processes, professional codes, and company policies that go beyond what the law strictly requires.

For an automated valuation system, good governance supported by soft ethics could include testing performance by neighbourhood and property type, measuring the size and direction of errors, requiring human review above financial thresholds, stress-testing the model in rising and falling markets, and pausing acquisitions when prediction uncertainty spikes. In my industry, professional obligations play a similar gap-filling role: a REALTOR®’s duties to clients exist regardless of what any algorithm outputs.

Soft ethics cannot replace regulation — companies can ignore voluntary principles when profit is at stake, and Zillow’s own incentives pushed toward growth. But it can close the gap while laws and industry standards catch up with the technology.

Conclusion: A Valuation Is a Judgment, Not Just a Number

The collapse of Zillow Offers was not one coding error. It was forecasting uncertainty, rapid expansion, operational limits, and — above all — excessive confidence in the ability to predict future home prices at scale. That complexity is exactly what makes it a valuable AI ethics case: even a sophisticated technology company with enormous amounts of real estate data could not safely automate property valuation, because a model that looks accurate in normal conditions can become unreliable the moment market behaviour changes.

AVMs are not useless. They offer speed, consistency, and a convenient starting point. The ethical danger begins when an estimate is treated as unquestionable truth, or when an organization gives a model more authority than its reliability justifies. Data quality, fairness, transparency, explainability, accountability, human oversight, governance, and soft ethics all point to the same conclusion: technology must remain answerable to the people affected by it.

In my own practice, I would never advise a seller to price a home based only on an online estimate. The number has to be tested against recent sales, property condition, neighbourhood differences, and the story behind every comparable. An algorithm can calculate patterns, but it does not walk through the house, speak with buyers, or understand why an offer night failed.

The algorithm may say what the house is worth. The ethical responsibility lies with the person or organization that decides whether to believe it.
